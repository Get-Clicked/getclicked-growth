Tangerine occupies a credible middle position in telehealth: $65 cash-pay with named providers, documentation services, and multi-state coverage. ## Competitor Gaps (Opportunities)

- **Documentation + employment/student health:** No national telehealth platform offers sick notes, return-to-work clearances, school physicals, or employment health docs via virtual visits. This is genuine white space.
- **Provider continuity:** Every major competitor delivers rotating-provider commodity care. A named provider practice builds trust and retention that marketplaces can't replicate.
- **Idaho is wide open:** No transparent flat-fee virtual competitor. Health systems don't publish cash-pay rates. DPC requires monthly commitment.
- **Rural WA is underserved:** Non-Medicaid patients in rural counties pay $79-$99+ or go without. $65 is compelling.
- **"No insurance" positioning is unowned regionally:** National platforms capture this intent, but nobody owns "cash-pay telehealth in [WA/ID/CO]."
- **Condition-specific content is fragmented:** "Online UTI treatment Idaho" or "strep throat telehealth Washington" has near-zero competition.
- **Simplicity as differentiator:** Every health system requires MyChart signup, insurance verification, or membership. "$65, no signup, talk to a provider in minutes" is a positioning gap.

## Industry Trends

- **Cash-pay telehealth is growing fast:** Sesame, GoodRx Care, Amazon One Medical pay-per-visit all launched or expanded in 2025-2026. The "skip insurance" movement is real.
- **Price compression at the bottom:** Sub-$50 visits are becoming commodity. Differentiation on price alone is unsustainable below $40.
- **Health systems are slow to adapt:** Hospital telehealth is designed for insured patients — opaque pricing, MyChart friction, limited hours. Massive experience gap vs. DTC telehealth.
- **AI triage is accelerating:** K Health (Mayo/MGH partnerships), Amazon, and others using AI to reduce provider time per visit. Drives costs down further.
- **Condition-specific DTC brands fragmenting the market:** Wisp (UTI), Hims/Hers (sexual health), Cerebral (mental health) — specialists are carving out verticals.
- **Rural telehealth adoption accelerating post-COVID:** Patient comfort with video visits is high; provider supply in rural areas remains low. Structural tailwind for virtual-first practices.

## Market Context

- **Addressable market:** Uninsured + underinsured + high-deductible plan holders + "insurance-exhausted" patients across WA, ID, CO
- **Key dynamic:** Price competition from national platforms at the bottom ($19-$49), health system dominance at the top ($79-$164), and a credible middle position for relationship-based, transparent, multi-service virtual care at $65
- **Seasonal patterns:** Cold/flu season (Oct-Mar) drives urgent care volume. Back-to-school (Aug-Sep) drives student health/physicals. Employment health is steady year-round.
- **Geographic priority:** Idaho first (least competitive), rural WA second (underserved + pricing gap), rural CO third (differentiation required vs. UCHealth/Simplified Care)
